    It responds to temperature changes inside the tank of your water heater and adjusts heating to match

    ## The Main Components of a Gas Control Valve

    Each part of the valve plays a specific role, and together they form a carefully coordinated system.
  </div>
  <div class="two-column">
    ### 1. The Solenoid

    The solenoid is an [electrically controlled](#where-does-the-electricy-come-from) valve inside the gas control assembly.

    * It opens to allow gas flow when conditions are safe
    * It closes instantly if a safety issue is detected

    Think of it as a gatekeeper â€” it only opens when the system confirms everything is operating correctly.
  </div>
  <div class="two-column">
    ### 2. The Main Gas Valve

    The main valve controls the volume of gas reaching the burner.
    When heat is needed:

    * The valve opens gradually
    * Gas flows at a controlled rate
    * The burner ignites and heats the water

    Once the desired temperature is reached, the valve closes again.
  This on-off cycling happens automatically throughout the day.
  </div>
  <div class="two-column">
    ### 3. The Thermocouple

    The thermocouple is a safety sensor and electrical power generator
    that detects whether the pilot flame is burning.

    * If the pilot is lit, it sends a continuous little electrical signal to the solenoid
    * If the flame goes out, the signal stops and the solenoid closes to shut off gas flow

    This prevents gas from escaping into your home unburned. Many times when a gas valve is not working properly,
    it is because the thermocouple has failed and just needs replacing.
  </div>
  <div class="two-column">
    ### 4. The Igniter (on some models)

    Older styles of water heaters provide you with a mechanismm by wich to light the gas burner manually.
    Others use an electronic ignition system that throws a spark at the gas coming out of the pilot light supply tube.
    The igniter is responsible for lighting the gas burner. Depending on the model, this may be:

    * A standing pilot light
    * An electronic ignition system

    [Lighting the pilot light](/articles/anatomy-of-a-water-heater-light-pilot/) is a simple process no matter the style you have.
  </div>
  <div class="one-column">
    ### 5. The Temperature Probe

    The temperature probe extends into the tank and monitors the water temperature.<br />
  When water cools below the set temperature:
    * The probe signals the control valve
    * The valve opens and heating begins

    When the water reaches the target temperature, the system shuts down automatically.
    This is how your water heater maintains steady, predictable temperatures throughout the day.

    ## Where does the electricy come from?
    **Your water heater is not plugged in and doesnt have a battery,
    so where does the power come from for the gas control valve to work?**

    The thermocouple generates a small amount of electricity using heat from the pilot light flame.
    When the flame heats the tip of the thermocouple,
    it creates a tiny electrical current through a process called
    the [thermoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoelectric_effect).
    This current signals the gas control valve that the pilot flame is burning safely,
    allowing gas to continue flowing to the burner.
    If the flame goes out:
    * the thermocouple cools,
    * the electrical signal stops,
    * and the gas valve automatically closes to prevent unburned gas from escaping.
    
    Itâ€™s a simple, reliable safety system that works without batteries or external power.
